Simple Short-Read Polishing Benchmarking

Posted on Sat 11 May 2019 in genomics

Long and noisy DNA sequencing reads (PacBio of Oxford Nanopore (ONT)) are now the most common data used for assembly of eukaryotic genomes. All such assemblies undergo polishing to correct errors present in primary contigs, and most use some form of short-read polishing to do this. Here, I present a simple comparison of a few tools available for the job.

Though Pilon might be the most popular tool for short-read polishing, there are some other easily available options. For one, Racon, a polisher original advertised for long-read polishing, also provides support for short-read polishing. Also, the Vertabrate Genomes Project provides a nice pipeline for using freebayes and bcftools for polishing.

In order to compare these 3 tools, I will use each of them to polish a noisy tomato genome assembled by wtdbg2 with ONT data. Here are some stats regarding this test assembly:

Species S. lycopersicum
Genome Size (bp) 734258459
Est. Rate of Heterozygosity (%) 0.04
Contig N50 (bp) 1705144
# Contigs 2822

The assembly was first assembled by wtdbg2, then underwent an extra round of polishing with the wtpoa-cns module. Therefore, the accuracy should be slightly better than the original wtdbg2 assembly. All polishing tools used default settings, with the exception of Pilon, which utilized the --fix-bases option.

% Correct k-mers¶

To compare the effectiveness of these tools, we will use the "cold" module of KAT. First, the program establishes a database of accurate k-mers from ample coverage of Illumina short reads. Then, for each k-mer in a contig, it queries this database. We can expect that errors in a contig will yield k-mers that do not appear in our accurate k-mer database. The percentage of correct k-mers is the percentage of k-mers from a contig which were present in our k-mer database. Let's process the KAT output for our three assemblies.

Consensus Accuracy¶

Note that the "% correct k-mer" for a given contig is not the same is the consensus accuracy (the percentage of correct bases). In fact, "% correct k-mer" should be quite a bit lower than the consensus accuracy. That is because a single error in a contig can yield many incorrect k-mers. Let's dig into this:

A single error will produce k consecutive incorrect k-mers (i.e. all of the k-mers that span that error). Two errors can induce as little as k +1 consecutive incorrect k-mers and as many as 2k consecutive incorrect k-mers. The former occurs when errors are adjacent and the latter occurs when the errors are separated by k bp.

To get an upper bound on consensus accuracy, we will assume that every k incorrect k-mers was produced by a single error. To get a lower bound, we will assume that the number of incorrect k-mers is equal to the number of errors + k - 1:

If $w = $ # of incorrect k-mers and $n$ = the contig length, we can express the lower bound and upper bound of consensus accuracy as follows:

$$accuracy_{upperbound} = 1 - \frac{\frac{w}{k}}{n}$$$$$$$$accuracy_{lowerbound} = 1 - \frac{w - k +1}{n}$$

Let's include these calculations in the dataframe, then plot (first upper bound, then lower bound):

There are a few assumptions being made here. Firstly, we note that with k=27 and an expected genome of around 1 Gbp, there is a low chance of k-mer collision. That is, we are unlikely to see an error k-mer in our k-mer database just by chance. We also assume that our k-mer database was established with sufficient coverage such that the probability of not observing a correct k-mer at least once in our database is quite low. We should also note that the upper bound of accuracy is a tighter bound than the lower bound, since it is more likely that our errors will be spread out across the contigs rather than clustered together.

k=27 # k-mer size used in KAT analysis
df["wrong_kmers"] = df["kmers_in_seq"] - df["non_zero_kmers"]
df["lower_bound_accuracy"] = (1 - ((df["wrong_kmers"] - k + 1)/df["seq_length"])) * 100
df["upper_bound_accuracy"] = (1 - ((df["wrong_kmers"]/k)/df["seq_length"])) * 100

Conclusions¶

Our analysis suggests that the Vertebrate Genomes Project recipe for polishing, which employs a combination of freebayes and bcftools, does the best job of polishing our wtdbg2 tomato genome. Though I don't include this analysis in this notebook, I also observe that the freebayes polished genome produces more accurate alignments to the SL3.0 Tomato reference genome relative to the other assemblies. We can also see that the consensus accuracy distributions for this freebayes polished assembly look quite good, even after just one round of polishing.
